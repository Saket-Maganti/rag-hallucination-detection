Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) is built on the premise that better
retrieval yields more faithful answers. We show this premise is unreliable and
can be actively dangerous. In a controlled study spanning 6,050+ queries across
SQuAD and PubMedQA, two generator families (Mistral-7B and Llama-3-8B), and
four retrieval configurations, we identify a \emph{refinement paradox}: naive
post-retrieval refinement raises local matching scores while reducing answer
faithfulness from 0.7995 to 0.6407 on SQuAD and introducing 10.0\%
hallucination by fragmenting otherwise coherent evidence. The mechanism is
context coherence degradation---splitting a broad passage into a narrow
high-similarity fragment removes the connective evidence that generators rely
on to produce grounded answers. Our selective refinement strategy (HCPC-v2)
recovers faithfulness to 0.7874 with 0\% hallucination by intervening on only
16.7\% of queries through an AND-gated detection policy with rank protection
and contiguity-preserving merge-back. On PubMedQA, where a general-domain
encoder is mismatched to the biomedical corpus, RAG itself becomes harmful:
hallucination rises from 8.0\% to 20.0\% for Mistral and from 4.0\% to 10.0\%
for Llama-3 relative to zero-shot prompting. Variance analysis reveals that
chunk size is the dominant design variable among the parameters tested,
accounting for 90.1\% of between-factor faithfulness variance on SQuAD
($F = 19.72$, $p < 0.001$, Cohen's $d = 1.41$), whereas prompt strategy
explains $< 0.1$\%. We formalize the mediating role of context
quality through the Context Coherence Score (CCS), a lightweight
retrieval-time diagnostic computed from pairwise chunk embedding similarities.
CCS consistently separates high-coherence retrieval sets (0\% hallucination)
from low-coherence sets that precede generation failures, providing a
practical guard-rail for RAG deployment without requiring answer-level
evaluation.
