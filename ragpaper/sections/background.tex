\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:bg}

\paragraph{The standard RAG pipeline.}
A modern RAG system~\citep{lewis2020rag,gao2024ragsurvey} threads four
stages: a dense retriever (e.g.\ MiniLM, BGE, E5) returns the top-$k$
passages by query--passage similarity; an optional cross-encoder
reranker rescores them~\citep{nogueira2019passage}; an optional
refinement module further filters or compresses each retained passage
(e.g.\ extractive sentence selection, query-conditioned summarization,
or learned compression~\citep{xu2024recomp}); and a generator
produces the final answer conditioned on the assembled context. Each
stage individually is reported to improve task performance on standard
benchmarks~\citep{karpukhin2020dpr,izacard2022few,xiong2024embedder}.

\paragraph{Decoder-side correction.}
Two influential lines move the correction \emph{into} the decoder.
Self-RAG~\citep{asai2024selfrag} fine-tunes the generator to emit
``reflection tokens'' that decide whether to retrieve, retrieve again,
or stop, paying a fine-tune-time cost and a $2\text{--}3\times$
inference latency cost. CRAG~\citep{yan2024crag} introduces a
lightweight retrieval evaluator that triggers web search when document
relevance is judged poor; it pays an external-call latency cost. Both
take the position that the generator (or a learned auxiliary) is the
right place to fix retrieval failures. We instead show that a simple
retrieval-time signal (CCS) flags the same failures with no decoder
work and no fine-tuning.

\paragraph{Hierarchical retrieval.}
RAPTOR~\citep{raptor2024} clusters retrieved passages into a tree and
retrieves from both leaves and LLM-summarised internal nodes,
producing higher-coherence summary contexts at the cost of an offline
LLM-summarisation pass. We compare against RAPTOR directly
(\S\ref{sec:robustness}); HCPC-v$2$ matches or beats it on $2/3$
datasets at roughly an order-of-magnitude lower indexing cost.

\paragraph{Faithfulness evaluation.}
Faithfulness --- whether a generated answer is supported by the
retrieved context --- is typically assessed via NLI scoring of the
answer against the retrieved documents~\citep{honovich2022true,laban2022summac,
es2024ragas}, occasionally complemented by human annotation. We
follow this standard, using a DeBERTa-v$3$ NLI scorer
(cross-encoder/nli-deberta-v$3$-base) released with
SummaC~\citep{laban2022summac}, and report a hallucination rate at
the conventional $0.5$ threshold.

\paragraph{Coherence in NLP.}
Discourse coherence has a long history in classical text
generation~\citep{li2014coherence,mesgar2018neural,nguyen2021conditional}
but has not, to our knowledge, been quantified as a deployment-time
RAG signal. \citet{shi2023large} show that LLMs are distracted by
irrelevant context but do not consider the case where each individual
passage is relevant yet the \emph{set} is internally fragmented ---
which is the regime our work targets. \citet{liu2024lostmiddle} document
position-dependent context utilization in long contexts; coherence is
orthogonal to position effects, and our results hold at top-$k$
configurations small enough that position bias is negligible.

\paragraph{What we add.}
The closest existing work flags retrieval failures via the
\emph{generator's} downstream behavior (Self-RAG, CRAG) or via
hierarchical context restructuring (RAPTOR). We make three additions:
(i) we identify a specific failure mode --- the refinement paradox ---
that the standard pipeline systematically produces; (ii) we provide a
generator-free retrieval-time signal (CCS) that detects it; and
(iii) we ship the diagnostic as a deployment policy (HCPC-v$2$) that
recovers faithfulness at one pairwise-similarity computation per
query, with no fine-tuning, labels, or auxiliary model calls.
