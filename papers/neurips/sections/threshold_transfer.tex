\section{Threshold Transfer}
\label{sec:threshold_transfer}

% POLISH P2-A: Added a worked caption example so recovery scores above 1 and
% below 0 are interpretable without rereading the normalization equation.
Thresholded retrieval policies can look strong when the threshold is tuned and
evaluated on the same dataset. We test transfer by tuning \(\tau\) on each of
five datasets and evaluating on all five. For evaluation dataset \(d\) and
threshold \(\tau\), recovery is
\[
\mathrm{Recovery}(d,\tau) =
\frac{\mathrm{Faith}_{\mathrm{gate}}(d,\tau) -
\mathrm{Faith}_{\mathrm{HCPC}\mbox{-}\mathrm{v1}}(d)}
{\mathrm{Faith}_{\mathrm{baseline}}(d) -
\mathrm{Faith}_{\mathrm{HCPC}\mbox{-}\mathrm{v1}}(d)}.
\]
Scores can exceed 1 when the gated policy exceeds the baseline relative to
HCPC-v1, and can be negative when transfer is worse than HCPC-v1 under this
normalization.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\small
\caption{Threshold-transfer recovery score. SQuAD, PubMedQA, and
NaturalQuestions show large in-domain versus off-diagonal gaps; TriviaQA
and HotpotQA do not. \emph{Worked example.} On PubMedQA, baseline
$=0.559148$, HCPC-v1 $=0.562581$, gated at $\tau{=}0.4$ $=0.557595$
(table rounded to three decimals); recovery $=
(0.557595-0.562581)/(0.559148-0.562581)=1.452$. Because HCPC-v1 happens
to outperform the dense baseline on PubMedQA, this above-$1$ score means
the gated policy fell $45.2\%$ of $|\text{baseline}-\text{HCPC-v1}|$
below the baseline; a negative score means the gated policy underperforms
HCPC-v1 under this normalization.}

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The result is uneven transfer, not a universal failure. Some datasets show a
large in-domain advantage, while others do not. Reporting only the tuned
diagonal would make the policy look more stable than the off-diagonal evidence
supports.
